There are things a Vulcan does not say. Five times Spock swallowed the words before they could surface, and one time — one unguarded, illogical, perfectly Human time — he didn't.
Author's Note: Classic trope, I know. I don't care. The boys deserve it. Set loosely during the five-year mission, no specific episode timeline. Thanks to my beta T'Vere for the Vulcan vocabulary check. 🖖
I. On the Bridge, Stardate 2817.4

The asteroid hit without warning.

One moment the bridge was all routine chatter and the soft pulse of consoles; the next, a shockwave rolled the Enterprise forty degrees to port and hurled James Kirk from his command chair into the navigation console. Uhura caught her own station. Chekov yelped. Sulu's hands never left the helm.

Spock was at Kirk's side before the ship had finished shuddering.

Jim. Are you injured?

Kirk looked up from the deck, hair in his eyes, grinning the infuriating way he always did when he was bleeding. "I'm fine, Spock."

Spock catalogued the laceration above his eyebrow, the angle of his left wrist, the dilation of his pupils. He calculated: minor concussion, probable. He noted the warmth of Kirk's arm beneath his hand where he had steadied him.

I was afraid.

He did not say it.

You should report to sickbay, Captain.
* * *
II. Sickbay, Stardate 2831.6

McCoy always found reasons to keep Kirk overnight on a biobed. Spock found this medically unnecessary in approximately sixty percent of cases. He arrived at 2300 hours with a data padd — purely for efficiency; there were reports requiring Kirk's signature — and found the captain asleep, frowning slightly at nothing.

Spock set the padd on the side table. He remained standing because there was no logical reason to sit. He stood for eleven point four minutes.

Kirk's frown eased.

You look younger when you are not carrying the ship.

Spock straightened the edge of the padd so it was parallel to the table. He left.

* * *
III. The Rec Room, 0130 Hours

They played chess the way other people argued — passionately, without quarter, until the board was a ruin and whoever had lost was smiling anyway. Tonight Kirk had sacrificed his queen for a trap that very nearly worked, and Spock had spent six moves dismantling it with something he refused to identify as reluctant admiration.

You enjoyed that.
I do not enjoy chess. I find it intellectually stimulating.
Spock. You're enjoying yourself right now.

Spock moved his rook. "Check."

Kirk laughed — a full, unguarded sound that he never used on the bridge — and Spock looked at the board and thought: I would play every night of this mission and count it sufficient.

He said: "Your move, Captain."

* * *
IV. The Surface of Daran V

There had been a cave-in. Forty minutes. Spock had spent them calculating oxygen supply and structural stress tolerances and not — not — thinking about the fact that Kirk was on the wrong side of the rubble.

When the rescue team broke through, Kirk came out of the dust grinning, carrying an injured colonist over one shoulder and telling a joke to the people he'd been trapped with. Of course he was.

He stopped when he saw Spock's face.

Hey. I'm alright.
I am aware.

Kirk searched his face for a moment. Whatever he found there made something in his expression go careful and soft. He put his hand, briefly, on Spock's shoulder — and Spock felt it through three layers of uniform like a live circuit.

Do not do that again. Do not ever do that again. I cannot —

"Good work coordinating the rescue," Kirk said.

"Thank you, Captain."

* * *
V. The Mind Meld, Stardate 3012.9

The meld was necessary. The ambassador was dying and the information he carried would die with him unless someone could receive it. Spock volunteered without being asked.

He had not anticipated how much of himself the meld would carry the other direction. When he surfaced he sat for a long moment with his eyes closed, reassembling.

Kirk's hand was on his arm.

Spock. You back?

// I am here. I am — yes. I am here. //

"I am." He opened his eyes. Kirk was kneeling in front of him, close enough that Spock could see the gold flecks in the hazel of his irises. I know what you look like from this distance now. I will always know.

Are you sure? You looked—
I am certain, Jim. I am quite certain.

Kirk held his gaze a beat too long, nodded, and stood. The warmth of his hand left Spock's arm. Spock did not watch him go.

He did.

* * *
+I. The Captain's Quarters, 2317 Hours

The chess match had gone late, as they often did, and the brandy Kirk had produced from some improbable cabinet recess had not helped either of them count the hour. The board between them was a stalemate neither had moved to break.

I keep thinking about Daran V. The way you looked when I came out of that tunnel. I've seen that look on people before, Spock. I just never expected to see it on you.

The ship breathed around them. Somewhere in the corridor a door opened and closed. Spock thought, with perfect clarity: I have suppressed this for two years, four months, and seventeen days. Vulcan discipline is supposed to be sufficient for any circumstance.

Apparently this was not any circumstance.

Jim. I find that I — I have been aware for some time that my regard for you has exceeded the parameters appropriate to our professional relationship. I do not expect reciprocity. I wished only for you to know.

Silence.

Then Kirk reached across the chess board — the stalemate, the patient impasse of it, all those untaken pieces — and put his hand over Spock's.

Spock. You complete idiot.

He said it the way someone says I love you, when they have been waiting two years, four months, and seventeen days to say it back.
